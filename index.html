Ortolan

Generate verifiably random 256-bit keys using dice (or coins) — no electronics, no software RNG, no trust required

Photo: Pierre Dalous

Why physical randomness? Digital random number generators have trust problems. The NSA backdoored Dual_EC_DRBG for seven years. Debian's OpenSSL generated breakable keys for 20 months. Intel's RDRAND is unauditable silicon. Many devices — especially embedded systems, hardware wallets, and air-gapped machines — can't guarantee quality randomness at all.

The fix: trade randomness from mathematics on a deterministic system for something in the physical world. Dice under a blanket. You can see every roll. No firmware, no supply chain, no trust required.

Why "Ortolan"? The ortolan bunting is a small bird traditionally eaten under a napkin. This tool recommends generating your password under a blanket for visual privacy. The bird is now protected by the EU — please don't eat them.

Design — Coin

The alphabet is 32 characters: the 26 lowercase letters a–z plus 6 digits 0–5. Since 25 = 32, every group of 5 flips maps to exactly one character with zero waste. The final character uses a 1-bit table. A literal . is inserted every 5 characters for readability — dots carry no entropy but can be left in the password.

Why a Coin?

Every digital random number generator you might use instead — /dev/urandom, Intel's RDRAND instruction, a hardware wallet's onboard TRNG — is a black box. You cannot observe or verify the entropy source. History shows this trust has been catastrophically misplaced:

Notable digital RNG failures
Dual_EC_DRBG (2006–2013) — A NIST-standardized PRNG contained a kleptographic backdoor. NSA spent ~$250M/year on its Sigint Enabling Project to influence commercial product designs, and reportedly paid RSA Security $10M to make Dual_EC_DRBG the default in BSAFE. Withdrawn from NIST SP 800-90A after the Snowden disclosures.

Debian OpenSSL (2006–2008) — A well-intentioned code change to suppress a Valgrind warning removed two lines from OpenSSL's md_rand.c, reducing entropy from 128+ bits to ~15 bits (the process ID). Every OpenSSL-generated SSL/SSH key on Debian-based systems for 20 months was trivially breakable. CVE-2008-0166.

Intel RDRAND — Processes raw entropy through on-chip AES-CBC-MAC whitening, making output fundamentally unauditable. Linux kernel developer Theodore Ts'o resisted pressure to rely on RDRAND alone. The CrossTalk vulnerability (CVE-2020-0543) later showed RDRAND outputs leaked across CPU cores via a shared staging buffer.

A coin in your pocket cannot be backdoored. You can observe every flip. The physics are well-understood (see Security Notes below). No firmware, no supply chain, no trust required.

Procedure — Coin

  1. Flip your coin 5 times and write down the bits left to right (e.g., HTTHT → 10010).
  2. Look up the 5-bit string in Table A to find the character. Write it down.
  3. Repeat steps 1–2 for a total of 51 groups (255 flips, characters 1–51).
  4. Flip 1 more time. Look up the single bit in Table B for the 52nd character.
  5. Transcribe your 52 characters, inserting a . every 5 characters for readability.

Coin Security Notes

Coin bias: 50.8% same-side — negligible for key generation.  Diaconis, Holmes & Montgomery ("Dynamical Bias in the Coin Toss," SIAM Review 49(2), 2007) showed that human thumb-flips cause the coin to precess (wobble), spending slightly more flight time with the starting face up. They predicted P(same side) ≈ 0.51. Bartoš et al. confirmed this empirically with 350,757 flips by 48 people using 46 different currencies ("Fair coins tend to land on the same side they started," arXiv:2310.04153, 2023; JASA 2025): measured P(same side) = 0.508, 95% CI [0.506, 0.509]. Crucially, P(heads) = 0.500, 95% CI [0.498, 0.502] — no coin denomination or country showed a heads-vs-tails bias when flipped. The entropy cost of a 0.508 bias is ~0.00019 bits per flip, or 0.047 bits over a full 256-flip sequence — completely negligible.
Always FLIP, never SPIN.  Spinning a coin on a table introduces large, coin-specific biases driven by physical asymmetry. Spinning a US penny on a table lands tails roughly 55% of the time. The largest dataset, 20,422 spins by students of Robin Lock at St. Lawrence University (Chance News 11.02, 2002), measured 45.0% heads. Gelman & Nolan (The American Statistician 56(4), 2002) discuss the physics of why spinning introduces such biases. Polish researchers Gliszczyński & Zawadowski found the Belgian €1 coin landed heads ~56% when spun. These are entropy-destroying biases. Flip the coin with your thumb and catch it in your hand. Never let it spin or bounce on a surface.
Von Neumann extractor (optional debiasing).  Von Neumann (1951) proved a beautifully simple technique: flip twice. If the pair differs (HT or TH), output the first result. If the pair matches (HH or TT), discard both and reflip. Because P(HT) = p(1−p) = P(TH) for any bias p, the output is perfectly unbiased regardless of the coin's actual bias, requiring only that flips are independent. The cost: ~4 flips per fair bit at best (25% efficiency). For 256-bit: budget ~1,024 flips. Peres (1992) improved efficiency by recursively processing discarded pairs.

Practical recommendation: The raw 0.8% bias is negligible for key generation. Use the von Neumann extractor only if you want a mathematically airtight guarantee, or if you suspect your particular flipping technique is unusually wobbly (Bartoš et al. found between-person variation with σ ≈ 1.6%).

Design — Dice

The alphabet is 36 characters: the 26 lowercase letters a–z plus 10 digits 0–9. Since 6 × 6 = 36, every roll of two dice maps to exactly one character with zero waste. A literal . is inserted every 5 characters for readability — dots carry no entropy but can be left in the password.

Always roll two dice together. First die = row, second die = column (or use different colors).

Why Dice?

The same RNG distrust that motivates coin-based generation applies to dice. But dice offer a key advantage: faster generation. Where coins require 256 individual flips, dice need only 50 simultaneous two-dice rolls.

Supply chain argument: Standard 6-sided dice are commodity items manufactured globally by hundreds of companies. Unlike casino dice (sold only to casinos) or specialty polyhedral dice, ordinary d6 dice are available at any toy store, pharmacy, or dollar store. An attacker cannot realistically intercept or tamper with the global supply of commodity dice. Grab two dice from any board game.

Dice Security Notes

Dice bias — negligible and unknowable to attackers.  Store-bought dice have minor imperfections: recessed pips remove more material from the 6-face than the 1-face, shifting the center of mass toward the 1-face (making 6 slightly more likely to land face-up). Campbell & Dolan (Western Kentucky University, 2019) found that detecting whether a die is unfair requires ~3,000 rolls — and even then, most commodity d6 dice tested fair.

The key insight: an attacker doesn't know which dice you used or their specific bias. Each die's imperfections are unique. Averaged across the global population of dice, biases cancel out. Unlike a backdoored RNG where the attacker knows the flaw, dice bias is local, small, and varies randomly between dice.
No debiasing needed.  Unlike coins (where the same-side bias is systematic across all coins), dice bias is idiosyncratic and tiny. A ~1–2% bias on any individual face, spread across 50 rolls, reduces entropy by a fraction of a bit — completely negligible for a 258-bit key. Von Neumann-style debiasing would waste rolls for no practical security gain.
Rolling technique matters.  Dice must bounce and tumble — don't drop flat or slide. Roll onto a hard surface with walls (dice tray, box lid, book). A dice tower provides consistent randomization. Roll both dice simultaneously; read left-to-right or use different colors to distinguish first vs. second die.

Shared Security Notes

Paper impressions — shred at least 7 pages.  Forensic electrostatic detection (ESDA, first commercialized in 1978 by Foster & Freeman) can recover pen indentations from up to 6 sheets beneath the page you wrote on. Seward's charge transport model (J. Forensic Sciences, 1999) explains the mechanism: paper-to-paper friction creates differential surface charges detectable by ESDA's corona wire. Under normal storage conditions, impressions can persist for decades.

Best practice: Write on a single loose sheet placed on a hard, non-porous surface (glass, hard clipboard). If you used a notepad, tear out and destroy the first 7 pages beneath the writing sheet. Oblique lighting reveals impressions 3–4 pages deep; ESDA goes to 7+.

Destruction method: Cross-cut shredding (DIN 66399 level P-4) is minimum. For high-security: wet pulp the pages (soak until fibers separate, then blend) or burn to ash. NSA guidance warns that even books thrown into a fire can remain half-readable after an hour. Strip-cut shredding has been reconstructed — most famously by Iranian students during the 1979 hostage crisis.
Visual eavesdropping — make a "bedsheet SCIF."  Shoulder-surfing attacks are effective at 0.9+ meters with the naked eye; modern phone cameras with 5× zoom extend this dramatically. NYU's IllusionPIN study found 21 of 21 unprotected shoulder-surfing attacks succeeded. Thermal cameras can read keypad heat signatures after entry.

Drape a bedsheet, blanket, or large towel over yourself and your workspace to create a makeshift SCIF (Sensitive Compartmented Information Facility). This blocks line-of-sight observation and defeats most camera angles. Do your coin flips or dice rolls and table lookups underneath. When ready, enter the password into your secure device (e.g., a Precursor or other air-gapped hardware) under the same cover. It looks silly. It works.
Electromagnetic emanations (TEMPEST).  Vuagnoux & Pasini (EPFL/LASEC, USENIX Security 2009) demonstrated recovery of 95% of keystrokes from PS/2 keyboards at up to 20 meters through walls. All 12 keyboard models tested were vulnerable. USB keyboards were also found vulnerable, though PS/2 keyboards were easiest to attack. Van Eck phreaking of CRT monitors was demonstrated at hundreds of meters in 1985. Even Faraday cages can be defeated: Guri et al.'s ODINI attack (IEEE TIFS, 2020) exfiltrated data through Faraday walls via low-frequency magnetic field modulation.

Implication: When entering your password into a device, do so in an interior room away from external walls and windows. Ideally use a device with a verified, auditable I/O path (e.g., the Precursor's FPGA-based architecture, where the entire CPU is instantiated from open RTL source). A bedsheet blocks visual eavesdropping but not EM emanations.
256-bit quantum resistance.  AES-256 retains ~128-bit effective security against Grover's quantum algorithm, making it NIST's recommendation for long-term sensitive data. Both coin (256 bits) and dice (258.5 bits) modes exceed this threshold.
Key derivation.  If using this password as input to software (not as a raw key), ensure the software applies a proper KDF (PBKDF2, Argon2, or HKDF). Standard in VeraCrypt, LUKS, KeePass, etc. The KDF stretches your high-entropy input and binds it to a salt, producing the actual symmetric key.
Prior art.  Physical randomness for key generation is well-established. Arnold Reinhold's Diceware (1995) uses five dice to generate passphrases from a 7,776-word list (~12.9 bits/word). The EFF published improved wordlists in 2016 (Joseph Bonneau). In the Bitcoin ecosystem, the Glacier Protocol, ColdCard, BlueWallet, and BitBox02 all support manual entropy input via dice or coins. David Vorick's "coins in a cup" method (Sia Foundation, 2020) uses modular arithmetic on five coins for entropy-preserving debiasing. The Glacier Protocol represents a thorough approach to physical key management.
