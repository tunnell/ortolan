Coin-Flip Password Generator

Generate verifiably random AES-128 or AES-256 keys using fair coin flips — no electronics, no software RNG, no trust required

Design

The alphabet is 32 characters: the 26 lowercase letters a–z plus 6 digits 0–5. Since 25 = 32, every group of 5 flips maps to exactly one character with zero waste. The leftover bits use a smaller table for the final character. A literal . is inserted every 5 characters for readability — dots carry no entropy but can be left in the password.

Why a Coin?

Every digital random number generator you might use instead — /dev/urandom, Intel's RDRAND instruction, a hardware wallet's onboard TRNG — is a black box. You cannot observe or verify the entropy source. History shows this trust has been catastrophically misplaced:

Notable digital RNG failures
Dual_EC_DRBG (2006–2013) — A NIST-standardized PRNG contained a kleptographic backdoor. NSA spent ~$250M/year on its Sigint Enabling Project to influence commercial product designs, and reportedly paid RSA Security $10M to make Dual_EC_DRBG the default in BSAFE. Withdrawn from NIST SP 800-90A after the Snowden disclosures.

Debian OpenSSL (2006–2008) — A well-intentioned code change to suppress a Valgrind warning removed two lines from OpenSSL's md_rand.c, reducing entropy from 128+ bits to ~15 bits (the process ID). Every SSL/SSH key generated on Debian-based systems for 20 months was trivially breakable. CVE-2008-0166.

Intel RDRAND — Processes raw entropy through on-chip AES-CBC-MAC whitening, making output fundamentally unauditable. Linux kernel developer Theodore Ts'o resisted pressure to rely on RDRAND alone. The CrossTalk vulnerability (CVE-2020-0543) later showed RDRAND outputs leaked across CPU cores via a shared staging buffer.

A coin in your pocket cannot be backdoored. You can observe every flip. The physics are well-understood (see Security Notes below). No firmware, no supply chain, no trust required.

Why not dice? Dice work well (see Diceware, Glacier Protocol), but you must purchase them — creating a supply chain you cannot fully verify. A weighted or shaved die is hard to detect by hand. Casino-grade precision dice (e.g., TCS John Huxley "Certified Perfects") are manufactured exclusively for casinos and not sold to consumers. Consumer "casino-style" dice are cosmetically similar but not held to the same ±0.05mm tolerance. A coin you already own has no purchase event to intercept or tamper with.

Procedure — 128-bit

  1. Flip your coin 5 times and write down the bits left to right (e.g., HTTHT → 10010).
  2. Look up the 5-bit string in Table A to find the character. Write it down.
  3. Repeat steps 1–2 for a total of 25 groups (125 flips, characters 1–25).
  4. Flip 3 more times. Look up the 3-bit string in Table B for the 26th character.
  5. Transcribe your 26 characters, inserting a . every 5 characters for readability.

Security Notes

Coin bias: 50.8% same-side — negligible for key generation.  Diaconis, Holmes & Montgomery ("Dynamical Bias in the Coin Toss," SIAM Review 49(2), 2007) showed that human thumb-flips cause the coin to precess (wobble), spending slightly more flight time with the starting face up. They predicted P(same side) ≈ 0.51. Bartoš et al. confirmed this empirically with 350,757 flips by 48 people using 44 different coins ("Fair coins tend to land on the same side they started," arXiv:2310.04153, 2023; JASA 2025): measured P(same side) = 0.508, 95% CI [0.506, 0.509]. Crucially, P(heads) = 0.500, 95% CI [0.498, 0.502] — no coin denomination or country showed a heads-vs-tails bias when flipped. The entropy cost of a 0.508 bias is ~0.00014 bits per flip, or 0.018 bits over a full 128-flip sequence — completely negligible. If you want mathematical perfection, use the von Neumann extractor (below).
Always FLIP, never SPIN.  Spinning a coin on a table introduces large, coin-specific biases driven by physical asymmetry. Spinning a US penny on a table lands tails roughly 55% of the time. The largest dataset, 20,422 spins by students of Robin Lock at St. Lawrence University (Chance News 11.02, 2002), measured 45.0% heads. Gelman & Nolan (The American Statistician 56(4), 2002) discuss the physics of why spinning introduces such biases. Polish researchers Gliszczyński & Zawadowski found the Belgian €1 coin landed heads ~56% when spun. These are entropy-destroying biases. Flip the coin with your thumb and catch it in your hand. Never let it spin or bounce on a surface.
Von Neumann extractor (optional debiasing).  Von Neumann (1951) proved a beautifully simple technique: flip twice. If the pair differs (HT or TH), output the first result. If the pair matches (HH or TT), discard both and reflip. Because P(HT) = p(1−p) = P(TH) for any bias p, the output is perfectly unbiased regardless of the coin's actual bias, requiring only that flips are independent. The cost: ~4 flips per fair bit at best (25% efficiency). For a 128-bit key: budget ~512 flips. For 256-bit: ~1,024 flips. Peres (1992) improved efficiency by recursively processing discarded pairs, approaching the information-theoretic optimum.

Practical recommendation: The raw 0.8% bias is negligible for key generation. Use the von Neumann extractor only if you want a mathematically airtight guarantee, or if you suspect your particular flipping technique is unusually wobbly (Bartoš et al. found between-person variation with σ ≈ 1.6%).
Paper impressions — shred at least 8 pages.  Forensic electrostatic detection (ESDA, first commercialized in 1978 by Foster & Freeman) can recover pen indentations from up to 7 sheets beneath the page you wrote on. Seward's charge transport model (J. Forensic Sciences, 1999) explains the mechanism: paper-to-paper friction creates differential surface charges detectable by ESDA's corona wire. Under normal storage conditions, impressions can persist for decades.

Best practice: Write on a single loose sheet placed on a hard, non-porous surface (glass, hard clipboard). If you used a notepad, tear out and destroy the first 8 pages beneath the writing sheet. Oblique lighting reveals impressions 3–4 pages deep; ESDA goes to 7+.

Destruction method: Cross-cut shredding (DIN 66399 level P-4) is minimum. For high-security: wet pulp the pages (soak until fibers separate, then blend) or burn to ash. NSA guidance warns that even books thrown into a fire can remain half-readable after an hour. Strip-cut shredding has been reconstructed — most famously by Iranian students during the 1979 hostage crisis.
Visual eavesdropping — make a "bedsheet SCIF."  Shoulder-surfing attacks are effective at 0.9+ meters with the naked eye; modern phone cameras with 5× zoom extend this dramatically. NYU's IllusionPIN study found 21 of 21 unprotected shoulder-surfing attacks succeeded. Thermal cameras can read keypad heat signatures after entry.

Drape a bedsheet, blanket, or large towel over yourself and your workspace to create a makeshift SCIF (Sensitive Compartmented Information Facility). This blocks line-of-sight observation and defeats most camera angles. Do your coin flips and table lookups underneath. When ready, enter the password into your secure device (e.g., a Precursor or other air-gapped hardware) under the same cover. It looks silly. It works.
Electromagnetic emanations (TEMPEST).  Vuagnoux & Pasini (EPFL/LASEC, USENIX Security 2009) demonstrated recovery of 95% of keystrokes from PS/2 keyboards at up to 20 meters through walls. All 12 keyboard models tested were vulnerable. USB keyboards were also found vulnerable, though PS/2 keyboards were easiest to attack. Van Eck phreaking of CRT monitors was demonstrated at hundreds of meters in 1985. Even Faraday cages can be defeated: Guri et al.'s ODINI attack (IEEE TIFS, 2020) exfiltrated data through Faraday walls via low-frequency magnetic field modulation.

Implication: When entering your password into a device, do so in an interior room away from external walls and windows. Ideally use a device with a verified, auditable I/O path (e.g., the Precursor's FPGA-based architecture, where the entire CPU is instantiated from open RTL source). A bedsheet blocks visual eavesdropping but not EM emanations.
128 vs. 256 bits.  AES-128 is NIST-approved (FIPS 197, SP 800-131A Rev. 2) and beyond any known classical brute-force attack (2128 operations is infeasible). Grover's quantum algorithm would halve effective security to ~64 bits. AES-256 retains ~128-bit effective security post-quantum, making it NIST's recommendation for long-term sensitive data. If your data must remain confidential for decades, choose 256-bit.
Key derivation.  If using this password as input to software (not as a raw key), ensure the software applies a proper KDF (PBKDF2, Argon2, or HKDF). Standard in VeraCrypt, LUKS, KeePass, etc. The KDF stretches your high-entropy input and binds it to a salt, producing the actual symmetric key.
Prior art.  Physical randomness for key generation is well-established. Arnold Reinhold's Diceware (1995) uses five dice to generate passphrases from a 7,776-word list (~12.9 bits/word). The EFF published improved wordlists in 2016 (Joseph Bonneau). In the Bitcoin ecosystem, the Glacier Protocol, ColdCard, BlueWallet, and BitBox02 all support manual entropy input via dice or coins. David Vorick's "coins in a cup" method (Sia Foundation, 2020) uses modular arithmetic on five coins for entropy-preserving debiasing. ICANN's DNSSEC Root Key Signing Ceremonies represent the gold standard for physical key management ritual. This reference card contributes a coin-specific binary-to-character encoding with zero-waste mapping.
